ThinkSTEAM

In a world where only 24% of women are in STEAM careers, I had the pleasure of working on a projects that promote young girls to get involved in STEAM and gain knowledge and experience through programs offered by ThinkSTEAM, a non-profit organization

The problem

The website interaction and class sign ups are very low and at risk of not having enough students to continue the programs.

The solution

Through research we discovered that parents lack information about STEAM's benefit and the website doesn't answer the needs parents have nor does it excite the parents and students to sign up for STEAM programs. We ended up redesigning the website to answer those needs.

Phase 1: Empathise (Research)

Proto Persona

The first thing we did was create a Proto Persona. Knowing ThinkSTEAM is a non profit organization offering programs for K-12 Students, we were able to make our assumption that it is their parents who would visit the website to sign their kids up for new programs.

Interview Plan

Interviews

After creating our proto persona we interviewed 6 parents to have depth into what their experience is like when it comes to STEAM. Each of the interviews gave us insights into what parents are thinking and how they make a decision about singing up for programs.

title

  1. STEAM knowledge was lacking.  Most parents heard of it, but didn't quite know all it stands for, where it can be implemented and why it is beneficial.
  2. Need for quick information.  Parents need to be able to read through the information with ease so they can make a fast decision. Speed/ease is also important when deciding on a program to sign up for.
  3. Sign up process need to be quick and easy. Parents need to be able to sign up for classes quickly and easily otherwise they postpone it for later, risking not completing the registration.

Survey

The interviews gave us a pretty good indication to what parents need, but we needed to confirm the results of our interviews using quantitative research that will confirm our findings. We conducted a survey with 28 parents who have kids in grades K-12. The survey confirmed some of the information we got through the interviews.

What we learned from our survey:

  1. The key take away was that awareness is key. 43% of parents didn't know at all what STEAM is and of those parents who had previous exposure to STEAM, many of them weren't quite sure what it stands for or why it is important.
  2. 50% of parents find art (the "A" in STEAM) very important.
  3. 86% of parents thought role models and inspirational examples of STEAM are important.

Heuristic Analysis

After getting our users's view, we conducted a Heuristic Analysis on ThinkSTEAM's website to see where it fell short. There were a lot of areas that needed to be modified for better UX which we noted for reference. The strongest motif that followed is that the overall look of the website was lacking the visuals to clarify what the website is about and get the parents and their children excited with the programs that they offer.

Competitor Analysis

How do ThinkSTEAM competitors present their information? Do they answer the needs of our users? We found out that each competitor website answer some of our users needs.

Phase 2: Define

At this point, we gathered a lot of information in our research and it was time to narrow it down and focus our findings to what our users are facing and what their challenges are.

Affinity Diagram

We used an Affinity Diagram to organize our interview notes into groups of similar information so we can analyze repeating data points.

User Insight Statement

Sarah needs better information about STEAM because she would like to support and encourage her daughter's interests in the field, however there is lack of representation around this approach in education (Science, Technology, Engineering, Art & Math).

Phase 3: Ideate

Once we discovered the challenges our users were facing when it comes to STEAM and to signing up for classes, it was time to brainstorm ideas around features we will include on the ThinkSTEAM website to improve its usability. We used the "I like, I wish, what if" method to gather all our ideas. We then voted on which features we would like to include.

Of the 8 chosen features, which improvements should we focus on? Each feature has a benefit, so we used a feature prioritization matrix to place each of those chosen features on a matrix of how complex/easy it will be to create, and how difficult it will be to develop. We spoke about each of the features and then voted on them again, this time knowing the effort it takes to develop.

Our selected features were:

  1. To help people appreciate how art and creativity contributed to other disciplines.
  2. Ability to sign up for a newsletter
  3. Easy class sign up - world wide.

We created a user flow to capture how each of the experiences will be. We iterated on the flow several times to ensure we ended up with a simple flow that will create a good user experience for our site visitors.
